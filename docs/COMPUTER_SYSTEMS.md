# 8-Bit Computer Systems (1974–1986)

### The Pioneers (1974–1976)
- [ ] Altair 8800 (1974) - The machine that started the microcomputer revolution.
- [ ] MOS Technology KIM-1 (1975) - A single-board computer used by hobbyists.
- [ ] Apple I (1976) - Wozniak's first design, sold as a bare circuit board.

### The "1977 Trinity" 
- [ ] Apple II series (1977) - Introduced color graphics and expansion slots.
- [ ] Commodore PET (1977) - The first "all-in-one" home computer with a built-in monitor.
- [ ] Tandy TRS-80 Model I (1977) - Affectionately known as the "Trash-80."

### Late 70s Expansion
- [ ] Exidy Sorcerer (1978) - One of the first to use plug-in ROM cartridges.
- [ ] Atari 400/800 (1979) - Advanced co-processors for graphics (ANTIC/CTIA) and sound (POKEY).
- [ ] Texas Instruments TI-99/4 (1979) - Technically used a 16-bit CPU but was architecturally constrained to 8-bit performance.

### The Early 80s Boom
- [ ] Sinclair ZX80 (1980) - The first computer to cost less than £100.
- [ ] Commodore VIC-20 (1980) - The first computer to sell over one million units.
- [ ] BBC Micro (1981) - Designed for the BBC Computer Literacy Project; very powerful for its time.
- [ ] Sinclair ZX81 (1981) - A streamlined, cheaper version of the ZX80.
- [ ] Commodore 64 (1982) - The best-selling single computer model of all time.
- [ ] ZX Spectrum (1982) - The heart of the UK software industry; famous for "color clash."
- [ ] Dragon 32/64 (1982) - A Welsh-built computer based on the Motorola 6809 CPU.

### Mid-80s Refinement
- [ ] Apple IIe (1983) - The longest-lived model in the Apple II series.
- [ ] MSX Standard systems (1983) - A standardized architecture led by Microsoft and ASCII Corp.
- [ ] Oric-1 / Atmos (1983) - A Z80-based rival to the Spectrum in Europe.
- [ ] Amstrad CPC 464 (1984) - Integrated tape deck and monitor; very popular in Europe.
- [ ] Apple IIc (1984) - A portable, "compact" version of the Apple II.
- [ ] Commodore Plus/4 (1984) - Intended for business but struggled to find an audience.
- [ ] Enterprise 64/128 (1985) - A powerful Z80 machine with unique graphics and sound capabilities.
- [ ] Commodore 128 (1985) - Featured three modes: C128, C64, and CP/M (via an onboard Z80).
- [ ] Amstrad CPC 6128 (1985) - Upgraded version of the 464 with 128KB RAM and a 3" disk drive.
- [ ] Tandy Color Computer 3 (1986) - The final evolution of the "CoCo" line.

# 16-Bit Computer Systems (1984–1992)

### The Motorola 68000 Powerhouses
- [ ] Apple Macintosh (1984) - The first commercially successful computer with a GUI and mouse.
- [ ] Atari ST Series (1985) - Famous for its built-in MIDI ports (used by musicians like Daft Punk and Fatboy Slim).
- [ ] Commodore Amiga 1000 (1985) - A revolution in multimedia with dedicated "custom chips" (Agnus, Denise, Paula).
- [ ] Sharp X68000 (1987) - A Japanese-exclusive powerhouse known for arcade-perfect ports (Capcom games).
- [ ] Commodore Amiga 500 (1987) - The "everyman's" 16-bit computer; the peak of the European demo scene.
- [ ] NeXT Computer (1988) - Steve Jobs' post-Apple project; used the 68030/040 and laid the foundation for macOS.

### The IBM PC & Compatibles (The Intel 80x86 Line)
- [ ] IBM PC/AT (1984) - Introduced the Intel 80286 and the 16-bit AT bus (ISA).
- [ ] Compaq Deskpro 386 (1986) - The first PC to use the 32-bit 80386, beating IBM to the punch.
- [ ] IBM PS/2 (1987) - Introduced the VGA graphics standard and the PS/2 mouse/keyboard ports.
- [ ] Tandy 1000 Series (1984) - PC compatibles famous for "Tandy Graphics" and 3-channel sound.

### The "Hybrid" & Late-Era 16-Bit Systems
- [ ] Acorn Archimedes (1987) - Technically 32-bit (ARM2), but competed in the 16-bit home market.
- [ ] Sinclair QL (1984) - Sinclair's professional Z80 successor; used a 68008 (16-bit internal/8-bit external).
- [ ] Apple IIGS (1986) - A 16-bit evolution of the Apple II using the W65C816S CPU.
- [ ] NEC PC-9801 (1982–1990s) - The dominant 16-bit architecture in Japan (Intel-based but not IBM compatible).
